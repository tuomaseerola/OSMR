7.1 The importance of replication

Empirical findings accumulate scientific value not only when they are first reported, but when they can be observed again under comparable conditions. A single study, however carefully designed, provides limited evidence on its own: its results may reflect a genuine effect, but they may equally reflect sampling variability, methodological idiosyncrasies, or the inadvertent influence of researcher decisions that go unreported. The credibility of scientific claims is substantially strengthened when independent researchers, working with fresh samples and potentially varying some procedural details, arrive at similar conclusions.

Concern about the replicability of published findings gained widespread attention following a large coordinated effort to reproduce results from experimental psychology. The Open Science Collaboration (2015) attempted to replicate 100 studies published in three leading psychology journals. The findings were sobering: fewer than half of the original effects replicated in the conventional sense of reaching statistical significance with the same direction of effect, and average effect sizes in replication studies were roughly half those of the original reports. Although the precise interpretation of these results has been debated, the project drew attention to the possibility that a substantial proportion of published findings may be less robust than they initially appeared.

Music psychology, as a discipline closely tied to experimental psychology, is not immune to these concerns. Studies in the field frequently involve small or convenience samples, rely on stimuli that are rarely shared or clearly specified, and draw on paradigms developed without cross-cultural validation. At the same time, music research has distinctive features that make replication both particularly valuable and particularly challenging. The relationship between acoustic features and musical experience depends on cultural background, familiarity, and individual differences in ways that may not generalise across populations. These considerations make transparent reporting of methods and materials—and a willingness to replicate key findings—an important part of responsible music research practice.

7.2 What is replication?

The term replication is used in a variety of ways in scientific discourse, and this variety can create confusion when researchers, reviewers, and readers evaluate claims about whether a finding has or has not replicated. Nosek and Errington (2020) propose a clarifying definition: a replication is an investigation that minimally alters the original circumstances while maintaining the same theoretical prediction. The goal of a replication is therefore not simply to reproduce the same numerical outcomes, but to test whether the same theoretical inference can be drawn from new evidence. Whether a replication succeeds or fails depends not on whether the statistics match, but on whether the conditions gave the original claim a genuine opportunity to be confirmed or disconfirmed.

This framing also clarifies an important distinction between two related but conceptually separate concepts: reproducibility and replicability (Nosek & Errington, 2020). Reproducibility refers to the ability to obtain the same analytical results from the same data using the same code and procedures. A failure of reproducibility—for example, when a reported analysis cannot be reproduced from the shared dataset—indicates a problem with the analysis or reporting rather than with the underlying empirical claim. Replicability, by contrast, refers to whether a new study, conducted with a fresh sample and potentially varying some procedural details, arrives at a similar conclusion. Failures of reproducibility and failures of replicability are distinct problems that call for different solutions.

In music research, both forms of transparency matter. Reproducible research requires sharing data, analysis code, and sufficient procedural detail for others to verify the reported results (see Chapter 5). Replicable research requires that original findings be robust enough to emerge in new samples and contexts, and that procedures are reported with enough specificity for others to implement them. A study that uses commercially licensed music recordings, withholds participant-level data, or describes stimuli only vaguely poses challenges for both reproducibility and replication—challenges that open materials practices are designed to address.

7.3 Types of replication

Replication studies are not a uniform category. The degree to which a replication attempt preserves or varies the methods of the original study has important consequences for what can be inferred from its results. Several typologies have been proposed; among the most commonly used is the distinction between direct and conceptual replication, extended by LeBel et al. (LeBel et al., 2018, 2019) into a more detailed taxonomy that grades replication studies by their procedural, sample, and contextual similarity to the original.

7.3.1 Direct replication

In a direct replication, the researcher aims to reproduce the original study as closely as possible, using the same or highly similar procedures, stimuli, measures, and participant population. The goal is to determine whether the original findings can be observed again under closely matched conditions. A direct replication that fails to reproduce the original result provides evidence that the effect may have been a false positive, that the original effect size was overestimated, or that some uncontrolled feature of the original study contributed to the outcome.

In music research, direct replication is complicated by the fact that stimuli are often inadequately reported or unavailable. When stimuli consist of commercially recorded music, sharing the original audio files may be legally constrained, and descriptions of excerpt boundaries, recording versions, or presentation parameters are frequently omitted from published reports. This underlines the importance of open materials practices: publishing stimuli directly, or reporting them in sufficient detail for others to approximate the original conditions, is a prerequisite for enabling meaningful direct replication.

Direct replication example

The infamous Mozart effect—the claim that brief exposure to Mozart’s music temporarily enhances spatial reasoning ability—was originally reported by Rauscher, Shaw, and Ky (1993) and attracted substantial media attention and policy interest. Subsequent attempts to replicate the effect produced inconsistent results. Chabris (1999) argued on the basis of available evidence that no reliable Mozart effect could be demonstrated, and a meta-analysis by Pietschnig, Voracek, and Formann (2010) examining data from over 39 studies concluded that while a small effect could be detected in aggregate, it was substantially smaller and more fragile than originally claimed, and likely reflected arousal or mood rather than a specific musical enhancement of spatial cognition. The Mozart effect thus illustrates both how a single unreplicated finding can achieve disproportionate public influence, and how systematic replication efforts are necessary to establish the robustness of empirical claims in music psychology.

7.3.2 Conceptual replication

A conceptual replication tests the same theoretical claim using different methods, stimuli, measures, or populations. Rather than reproducing the original study, the aim is to determine whether the underlying prediction holds when operationalised in a different way. Conceptual replications provide evidence for the generalisability of a finding but are more difficult to interpret when they fail: a conceptual failure to replicate could indicate that the original theory is wrong, or simply that the new operationalisation does not adequately capture the same construct.

In music psychology, conceptual replication frequently takes the form of cross-cultural studies that examine whether findings obtained with Western participants and Western musical materials generalise to listeners from different musical traditions. Cross-cultural studies of music and emotion perception, for example, have tested whether the emotional qualities attributed to musical stimuli are consistent across listeners with varying degrees of exposure to Western tonal music. Such studies do not reproduce the original conditions, but they test whether the same theoretical claim—that certain musical features reliably signal certain emotional qualities—holds across different contexts.

Adversarial collaboration, in which researchers holding opposing views design a study that both parties consider a fair test, is a related strategy that can strengthen the credibility of conceptual replications. By agreeing in advance on what evidence would support or refute each position, adversarial collaborators reduce the risk that a failed conceptual replication will be dismissed on methodological grounds [e.g., axt2026relationship; binnendyk2025overconfidence; matzke2015horizontal].

Conceptual replication example

The emotional associations of musical mode—the widely held claim that major keys sound happy and minor keys sound sad—have been subject to extensive conceptual replication across cultural contexts. While this association is robust among listeners with Western musical enculturation, studies examining listeners from non-Western musical traditions have produced more mixed results. Egermann et al. (2015) compared emotional responses to music in Western and Congolese participants and found that mode-based emotional attributions were substantially weaker in the Congolese sample, suggesting that the association may depend on enculturation rather than universal auditory features. This kind of cross-cultural work serves as a conceptual replication by testing whether the same theoretical claim holds in a population for which the original conditions—Western listeners, Western tonal music—do not straightforwardly apply.

7.4 Single studies and multi-lab replications

Replication studies vary not only in the degree to which they reproduce original methods but also in scale. A single-lab replication—conducted by one team in one location—can provide useful evidence about whether a finding holds in a new sample, but its conclusions are themselves limited by sample size and local context. A successful single-lab replication may still be a false positive; a failed one may reflect local conditions rather than a genuine failure of the original effect. Multi-lab replications, in which many independent research teams test the same hypothesis using a coordinated protocol, can provide substantially more reliable evidence, and additionally allow researchers to examine whether effect sizes vary across sites, samples, and cultural settings.

Large-scale collaborative replication initiatives have become increasingly common in psychology. The ManyLabs projects (Klein et al., 2014, 2018) coordinated replication attempts across dozens of sites and multiple countries, enabling systematic examination of replicability and cross-site variability. The Psychological Science Accelerator [PSA; Moshontz et al. (2018)] provides ongoing institutional infrastructure for large-scale, globally distributed psychological research, including studies that would be impractical for any single laboratory to conduct.

Music psychology has begun to engage with multi-lab and large-scale approaches, though coordinated multi-site replications remain relatively rare in the field. Cross-cultural studies offer one practical model: by collecting data at multiple geographically and culturally distinct sites, researchers can simultaneously test whether effects replicate across samples and assess the moderating role of cultural background. Large-scale online data collection has also created new opportunities to reach larger and more diverse samples than are typically accessible in single-laboratory research, making it more feasible to detect small or variable effects and to test the boundary conditions of theoretical claims.

Multi-lab replication example

Mehr et al. (2019) coordinated a large cross-cultural study testing claims about universals in human song, collecting behavioural data from participants across sixty societies, including communities with limited prior exposure to Western music. The study tested theoretical predictions about the formal properties of music that might be universal—such as the use of discrete pitches, small pitch intervals, and regular rhythmic organisation—and about the extent to which listeners unfamiliar with a song’s cultural origin can nonetheless infer its social function (e.g., lullaby vs. dance song). By coordinating data collection across sites with diverse populations and using a shared stimulus set, the project provided a substantially stronger and more generalisable test of universality claims than would have been possible within any single laboratory, while also documenting the considerable cultural variation in musical features that co-exists with apparent universals.

7.5 Qualitative research and replication

The discussion of replication in music research extends beyond quantitative experimental designs to include qualitative and interpretive approaches. Qualitative methods—including interviews, ethnographies, case studies, and thematic analysis—are well established in musicology, ethnomusicology, music education, and increasingly in music psychology. What replication means in these contexts requires careful consideration, since qualitative research is not typically designed to test hypotheses about population-level effects in the way that experimental studies are.

Tuval-Mashiach (2021) argues that replication in qualitative research is both meaningful and valuable, but requires a different conceptual framework. Because qualitative research is generally concerned with understanding the particular—the lived experience of specific participants, the meanings embedded in specific musical contexts—replication is not aimed at reproducing the same findings in a new sample. Instead, Tuval-Mashiach distinguishes between methodological replication, which applies the same or similar analytical procedures to a new sample to test whether comparable themes or patterns emerge, and theoretical replication, which tests whether theoretical propositions that developed in one context hold in a different context with different participants or settings. A third mode, reflexive replication, involves revisiting a previously analysed dataset with different analytic assumptions or frameworks, examining whether alternative interpretations are defensible.

These principles apply directly to qualitative and mixed-methods work in music research. Phenomenological studies of musical experience, interview-based investigations of music listening, or thematic analyses of the functions of music in everyday life may be difficult to replicate in any strict procedural sense, but their credibility is enhanced when researchers document analytical procedures transparently, provide access to data where ethically permissible, and situate their interpretations in relation to existing theoretical frameworks. Transparency about the research process—including decisions about sampling, coding scheme development, and interpretation—serves a function analogous to that of preregistration in quantitative research (see Chapter 9): it allows readers to evaluate how conclusions were reached and to assess whether the reported themes are likely to reflect the data rather than researcher expectations.

Qualitative replication example

DeNora (2000) used ethnographic interviews to document how individuals use music as a resource for emotion regulation, identity construction, and the management of everyday environments. The theoretical account that emerged from this work—of music as an affordance for self-regulation—has subsequently been examined using comparable qualitative approaches across diverse populations and cultural settings, including studies of music use among adolescents, older adults, and clinical populations. Although these follow-on studies were not designed as formal replications and varied considerably in their methods, together they constitute a form of theoretical replication: the same core propositions about music’s self-regulatory functions recur across studies using different samples, interview protocols, and analytic approaches. The consistency of these themes across contexts strengthens confidence in the underlying theoretical framework, even in the absence of the confirmatory statistical logic that governs quantitative replication.

7.6 Summary

Replication is fundamental to the credibility of scientific findings. For music research, engaging seriously with replication means attending both to whether existing findings can be reproduced from shared data and code, and to whether theoretical claims hold when tested in new samples and contexts. Both forms of scrutiny require a commitment to transparency in reporting methods, stimuli, and analytical decisions.

The diversity of replication types—direct, conceptual, single-lab, multi-lab—reflects the fact that replication is not a single practice but a family of related strategies for testing the robustness of empirical claims. Direct replications evaluate whether findings persist under closely matched conditions; conceptual replications assess whether theoretical claims generalise across different operationalisations and populations; multi-lab replications provide evidence about both average robustness and cross-site variability. Each approach has different strengths and limitations, and a mature research programme typically benefits from contributions of several kinds.

For music research, replication poses distinctive practical challenges. Stimuli are frequently underspecified or unavailable; participant samples are often small and culturally restricted; and musical experience depends on factors—cultural background, personal history, familiarity—that are difficult to control or match across studies. These challenges do not make replication impossible, but they do underline the importance of open materials practices and thoughtful stimulus reporting as preconditions for meaningful replication attempts.

Replication is not equally appropriate for all types of music research. For exploratory, interpretive, or practice-based work, formal replication may be neither feasible nor the right standard of evidence. In these cases, transparency about the research process—documenting analytical decisions, sharing data and materials where possible, providing reflexive accounts of interpretation—serves analogous purposes. Replication, like preregistration, is most valuable when the goal is to test predefined theoretical claims. It should be understood as one among several complementary open science practices that together strengthen the credibility, generalisability, and accessibility of music research.
