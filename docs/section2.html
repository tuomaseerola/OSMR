4  Availability of publications

4.1 Open publications

There are several routes for making publications openly available, each with different cost structures, embargo conditions, and implications for funder compliance. For a concise overview of Open Access (OA) publishing, see Suber (2012).

  • Gold OA: Publishing in a fully open-access journal where all content is immediately and permanently free to read without restrictions. Authors usually pay an Article Processing Charge (APC), through this varies widely by journal and discipline.
  • Hybrid OA: A subscription journal that allows individual articles to be made open access for a fee. Some funder mandates (e.g., Plan S, UKRI, NIH) increasingly require immediate public access to accepted manuscripts, with no embargo under updated policies, which limits how straightforward hybrid options are. Under plan S in particular, hybrid journals are generally only compliant when covered by transformative agreements or formal transition pathways.
  • Diamond / Platinum OA: Journals that are fully open access with no charges for authors or readers. These are typically supported by universities, societies, or library consortia. Several music journals follow this model (see below). For a broader overview, see Bosman et al. (2021).
  • Green OA (self-archiving): Depositing the accepted manuscript in a repository such as Zenodo, arXiv (see below), or an institutional repository. Policies differ by publisher, especially around embargo length and which versions can be shared: Jisc - Open policy finder can be used for checking journal-specific policies. In the EU, Horizon Europe encourages this route and expects immediate open access to accepted manuscrits via repositories.
  • APC costs and waivers: APCs range from a few hundred to several thousand USD or EUR. Some publishers offer waivers or discounts for researchers without funding or from low-income countries, so it is worth checking before assuming APC-based OA is not feasible.

4.2 OA publishing in numbers

Open access publishing has grown substantially over the past decade, though the landscape remains uneven. Between 2014 and 2024, the share of global scholarly articles made available via gold OA rose from 14% to 40%, while the subscription-only share fell from 70% to 54% (STM Association, 2024). This shift has been driven in part by funder mandates, but also by the rapid growth of APC-funded publishing, which carries its own equity concerns: annual global APC spending across six major publishers alone almost tripled from $910 million in 2019 to $2.5 billion in 2023 (Haustein et al., 2024).

4.3 Preprints

Preprints, i.e., versions of manuscripts shared publicly before or alongside formal peer review, are now a widely used mechanism for rapid and open dissemination. For practical guidance on posting and managing preprints across the publication pipeline, see Moshontz et al. (2021).

  • Where to post (varies by subdiscipline):

    • arXiv (categories cs.SD and eess.AS) for Music Information Retrieval (MIR), music technology, and Machine Leanring(ML)-adjacent work
    • PsyArXiv for music psychology
    • OSF Preprints for interdisciplinary work
    • Zenodo for general-purpose dissemination, including datasets and code alongside papers
    • SSRN for social science and related interdisciplinary work
    • HAL for computer science, engineering, humanities, and social sciences

  • Subdiscipline norms vary widely: Preprinting is well established in MIR and ML-adjacent music research, where posting on arXiv prior to or alongside ISMIR, ICASSP, or NeurIPS submissions is standard practice. Uptake is still uneven in music psychology and remains limited in musicology and music education, although adoption is gradually increasing.

  • Versioning: When a preprint is later published, the two versions should be explicitly linked (for example via DOI cross-references). Some repositories, such as PsyArXiv, support metadata linking that helps citation systems merge records across versions.

  • Journal prior-posting policies: Most journals now allow preprints, but conditions still vary regarding timing, licensing, and accepted manuscript versions. It is advisable to verify specific policies via Jisc - Open policy finder before submission.

  • Citation advantage of preprints: Several large-scale studies report that papers with an associated preprint tend to receive earlier and often higher citation counts compared to those without preprints, likely due to earlier visibility and dissemination (e.g., Serghiou & Ioannidis (2018), Fraser et al. (2020)).

  • Academic sharing platforms: Services such as ResearchGate and Academia.edu are often used to upload and circulate papers, including preprints. However, they are not formal preprint repositories and do not provide consistent long-term preservation or clear policy compliance guarantees. They function more as academic networking and sharing spaces than as archival infrastructure.

  • Secondary interpretation tools: Services such as arXivExplained and similar paper summarisation tools sit one step removed from publication. They help make preprints easier to read and explore, but they are not repositories and do not host canonical versions of research outputs. They are interpretation layers rather than publication infrastructure.
